# Academic Writing Skill

## Document Structure

### 1. Abstract (150-300 words)
- State the research question or objective
- Briefly describe the methodology
- Summarize key findings
- State the significance of the contribution
- Write this LAST, after the full paper is complete

### 2. Introduction (15-20% of body)
- Establish the research context and significance
- Review relevant prior work (brief — full review comes later)
- Identify the gap in existing knowledge
- State the research question or hypothesis clearly
- Outline the paper's structure

### 3. Literature Review (if separate; 20-25%)
- Organize thematically, not chronologically
- Critically analyze sources, don't just summarize
- Show how each source relates to the research question
- Identify consensus, disagreements, and gaps
- End by positioning your work in the existing landscape

### 4. Methodology (15-20%)
- Describe the approach in enough detail for replication
- Justify methodological choices
- Acknowledge limitations of the chosen approach
- For empirical work: describe data collection and analysis procedures

### 5. Results / Analysis (20-25%)
- Present findings objectively without interpretation
- Use tables, figures, and data visualizations where appropriate
- Report both significant and non-significant results
- Organize by research question or hypothesis

### 6. Discussion (15-20%)
- Interpret results in context of the research question
- Compare with existing literature
- Explain unexpected findings
- Discuss implications (theoretical and practical)
- Acknowledge limitations honestly

### 7. Conclusion (5-10%)
- Summarize the contribution
- Answer the research question directly
- Suggest directions for future research
- End with the broader significance

## Citation Rules

- Every factual claim must have a citation
- Use consistent citation style throughout (APA, MLA, Chicago, etc.)
- Cite primary sources whenever possible
- Paraphrase; direct quotes should be rare and justified
- Self-citation should be minimal and relevant

## Tone and Style

- Use third person and passive voice where conventional
- Avoid emotional language, superlatives, and hyperbole
- Define technical terms on first use
- Use hedging language appropriately: "suggests", "indicates", "may"
- Be precise: "67% of participants" > "most participants"

## Paragraph Structure

- One idea per paragraph
- Topic sentence → Evidence → Analysis → Connection
- Every paragraph should connect to the thesis
- Use transitional phrases between sections

## Common Pitfalls

- ❌ Making claims without citations
- ❌ Confusing correlation with causation
- ❌ Over-generalizing from limited data
- ❌ Burying the thesis in the middle of the introduction
- ❌ Using informal language or contractions
- ❌ Plagiarizing by paraphrasing too closely
