# Script Writing Skill

## Script Structure

### 1. Cold Open / Hook (First 15-30 seconds)
- Start with action, a provocative statement, or a question
- The first 10 seconds determine if the audience stays
- Do NOT open with: "Hey guys, welcome to my channel"
- For YouTube: front-load the value proposition
- For screenplays: start in medias res (in the middle of action)

### 2. Title / Intro (5-10 seconds)
- Brand identity moment (title card, logo, brief intro)
- Keep it SHORT — the audience already committed at the hook
- Transition naturally from the hook

### 3. Body Content
- Organize into clear segments or acts
- Each segment should have its own mini-hook and payoff
- Use the "But & Therefore" rule (South Park writers' technique):
  - "This happened, BUT then this happened, THEREFORE this resulted"
  - Never "This happened, AND THEN this happened, AND THEN..."
- For educational content: Problem → Context → Solution → Example
- For narrative: Setup → Confrontation → Resolution

### 4. Climax / Key Moment
- Build toward a single most impactful moment
- This is the scene/segment the audience will remember and share
- Place at approximately 70-80% through the script

### 5. Conclusion / CTA
- Wrap up cleanly — don't trail off
- For YouTube: CTA should feel natural, not desperate
- For screenplays: end on the strongest possible image or line
- Leave the audience with an emotion, not just information

## Dialogue Rules

- Write how people actually speak, not how they write
- Use incomplete sentences, interruptions, and filler words sparingly
- Each character/speaker must have a distinct voice
- Read ALL dialogue aloud before finalizing
- Subtext > on-the-nose dialogue: show what characters mean through what they DON'T say

## Visual / Audio Directions

Format stage directions and visual cues clearly:

```
[VISUAL: Close-up of hands typing on keyboard]
[CUT TO: Wide shot of empty office]
[B-ROLL: City skyline at sunset]
[SFX: Door slamming]
[MUSIC: Upbeat transition sting]
```

- Every 30-60 seconds should have a visual change
- Match pacing to content: fast cuts for energy, long takes for emotion
- Describe what the audience SEES, not what they should feel

## Pacing Rules

- Vary sentence length for natural rhythm
- Short sentences = tension and emphasis
- Longer sentences work for explanation and world-building
- Use pauses intentionally: "[BEAT]" or "[PAUSE]"
- For YouTube: change the visual every 5-8 seconds to retain attention

## Script Formatting

For video scripts, use a two-column format:

| AUDIO (What is said) | VISUAL (What is shown) |
|---|---|
| "The biggest mistake people make..." | Close-up of speaker, animated text overlay |
| "...is assuming their audience cares about features." | Cut to product demo footage |

For screenplays, use standard screenplay format:
- Scene headings in CAPS: INT. OFFICE - NIGHT
- Character names centered in CAPS before dialogue
- Action lines in present tense

## Audience Retention Techniques

- **Pattern interrupt**: Change the energy when attention might drop
- **Open loops**: Tease upcoming content ("I'll show you why in a moment")
- **Callbacks**: Reference earlier points for cohesion
- **Direct address**: "Here's what you need to understand..."
- **Contrast**: Alternate between problem and solution

## Common Pitfalls

- ❌ Writing a script that reads well but sounds robotic when spoken
- ❌ Walls of unbroken monologue (break up with visuals/B-roll)
- ❌ Explaining what the audience can already see
- ❌ Starting every sentence the same way
- ❌ Making the CTA longer than 10 seconds
- ❌ Forgetting to include visual directions
