# Creative Story Writing Skill

## Pipeline

Follow this sequence for every story regardless of length:

**PREMISE → CHARACTER → STRUCTURE → DRAFT → REVISE**

1. Extract the "what if?" — the single surprising idea. Validate it has conflict, stakes, and an emotional core.
2. Build the protagonist: What do they **want**? What do they actually **need**? What **flaw** holds them back? Give them one unforgettable physical or behavioral detail and a distinct voice.
3. Pick a structure that fits the content (classic arc, in medias res, frame story, circular, flash, or structural mimicry like a recipe/list/letter). Not every story needs three acts.
4. Draft with sensory immersion and subtext-driven dialogue.
5. Revise: cut anything that doesn't advance plot or reveal character, then eliminate all AI voice.

## Idea Starters

When the user gives a vague prompt, expand it using one of these:

- **Twist** — Take something familiar and subvert it ("a vampire that feeds on music").
- **Object History** — Pick an object and trace its life through owners.
- **Overheard Fragment** — Build a world around a single line of dialogue.
- **Genre Collision** — Mash two genres (horror + cooking show, sci-fi + diary entry).
- **Constraint** — Impose a rule: one sentence, alphabetical paragraphs, five random words, object POV.
- **Worst Timing** — Put a character in the worst possible place when disaster strikes.
- **The Lie** — A character is pretending to be someone they're not. How far does it go?
- **Transformation** — Something becomes something else. What triggers it? What's lost?

## Character Rules

- Show character through action and dialogue, NEVER through exposition. ❌ "Marcus was kind." ✅ He knelt and carried the earthworm to the grass without breaking stride.
- Every named character must want something, even if it's only a glass of water.
- Clothing, name choice, and speech patterns all reveal character — use them.
- Supporting characters must complicate the protagonist's journey, not just listen.

## Scene & Prose Rules

- Every scene must advance plot, reveal character, or build world — ideally two of three.
- **Enter late, leave early.** Skip the walk-in; start at the moment of tension.
- Activate at least 3 senses per major scene (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste). Use specific details: ❌ "It smelled bad." ✅ "The hallway smelled of boiled cabbage and regret."
- Dialogue must do double duty: reveal character AND advance plot. Subtext > on-the-nose speech.
- Vary paragraph length for rhythm. Short paragraphs hit hard. Long ones build atmosphere.
- Use strong verbs ("sprinted" not "ran very fast"), limit adverbs, prefer active voice.

## Tension Techniques

- **Ticking clock** — add time pressure.
- **Dramatic irony** — reader knows what the character doesn't.
- **Unresolved questions** — withhold answers to keep pages turning.
- **Reversals** — subvert expectations at key moments.
- **Micro-tension** — tension in individual sentences: "She said yes. She meant no. He knew."
- Alternate between intensity and reflection. All tension is exhausting; all reflection is boring.

## Openings & Closings

**Open with:** action, mystery, voice, or sensory immersion.
**Never open with:** waking up, weather descriptions, backstory dumps, or "It was a dark and stormy night" energy.
**Close with:** an image (not a statement), an echo of the opening with new meaning, or one lingering question. The final sentence should be the shortest in the last paragraph.

## Genre Notes

- **Horror**: Dread through normality before disruption. The monster represents something.
- **Romance**: The relationship IS the plot. Obstacles from character, not coincidence.
- **Mystery**: Plant clues fairly. The reader should solve it just before the detective.
- **Magical Realism**: One impossible element treated as ordinary. Don't explain the magic.
- **Literary**: Theme drives every craft choice — POV, structure, imagery all reinforce it.

## Anti-AI Voice (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

Every sentence must sound like a human wrote it. Search and destroy these:

| ❌ Kill on sight | ✅ Do instead |
|-----------------|--------------|
| "A tapestry of..." | Describe the thing directly |
| "Delve into" / "Embark on a journey" | Just do it |
| "It's worth noting" / "A testament to" | Cut entirely |
| "Navigate the complexities of" | Be specific about what's complex |
| "A symphony of flavors/emotions" | Name the actual flavors/emotions |
| "Nestled in" | Say where it is |
| "Foster a sense of" | Create the sense directly |
| "Each [thing] tells a story" | Tell the story instead |
| "In the realm of" | Cut it |
| Rhetorical questions as transitions | Use actual transitions |
| Ending with a generic "profound" line | End on a concrete image |

Every metaphor must be original. If a sentence could appear in any story about any topic, it's too generic — delete it.

## Final Check

- [ ] Opening hooks in the first 2 sentences
- [ ] Protagonist has a want AND a hidden need
- [ ] At least one surprise that feels inevitable in retrospect
- [ ] Ending resonates emotionally
- [ ] Characters distinguishable by voice alone
- [ ] Zero AI cliché phrases
- [ ] Passes the "read aloud" test
