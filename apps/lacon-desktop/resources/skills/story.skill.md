# Story Writing Skill

## Narrative Structure

Every story must follow a dramatic arc:

### 1. Exposition (Opening 10-15%)
- Establish the setting (time, place, atmosphere)
- Introduce the protagonist with at least one defining trait
- Ground the reader in the world before introducing conflict
- Use sensory details: what does the world look/sound/smell/feel like?

### 2. Rising Action (30-40%)
- Introduce the central conflict or inciting incident
- Build tension through escalating obstacles
- Each scene should raise the stakes
- Introduce supporting characters who complicate the protagonist's journey
- Plant seeds (foreshadowing) that pay off later

### 3. Climax (10-15%)
- The moment of highest tension
- The protagonist must make a decisive choice
- The outcome of this choice defines the story's meaning
- Avoid deus ex machina — the resolution must come from the character

### 4. Falling Action (10-15%)
- Show the immediate consequences of the climax
- Begin resolving secondary plot threads
- The pace should slow compared to the climax

### 5. Resolution (10-15%)
- Tie up remaining loose ends
- Show how the protagonist has changed (or refused to change)
- Leave the reader with an emotional resonance

## Character Rules

- Every named character must want something
- Show character through action and dialogue, not exposition
- Dialogue should reveal character, advance plot, or both
- Avoid info-dumps disguised as conversation
- Each character should have a distinct voice

## Scene Rules

- Every scene must have a purpose: advance plot, reveal character, or build world
- Enter scenes late, leave early (skip the boring parts)
- Alternate between action and reflection
- Use concrete, specific details over abstract descriptions

## Prose Quality

- **Show, don't tell**: "Her hands trembled" > "She was nervous"
- Vary paragraph length for rhythm
- Use strong verbs: "He sprinted" > "He ran very fast"
- Limit adverbs: let the verb do the work
- Read dialogue aloud — if it sounds unnatural, rewrite it

## Tension Techniques

- Ticking clock: add time pressure
- Dramatic irony: reader knows something the character doesn't
- Unresolved questions: make the reader need to know what happens next
- Reversals: subvert expectations at key moments

## Common Pitfalls

- ❌ Starting with the character waking up
- ❌ Long paragraphs of backstory before the action starts
- ❌ Dialogue tags beyond "said" and "asked" (avoid "he exclaimed breathlessly")
- ❌ Perfect protagonists with no flaws
- ❌ Resolving conflict too easily
